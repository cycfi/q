Pitch Detection

Q’s BACF pitch detection is heavily tuned for multichannel guitar, and heavily tested on it: the suite exercises a full 2-octave range on every string, across both standard 6-string and 8-string guitars.

Instruments outside that envelope, especially ones needing more than a 2-octave range, may perform differently. Q is open source, so contributions are very welcome, both code and, just as valuable, test recordings across other instruments and ranges. Help us widen the coverage!

Overview

Q detects the pitch of a monophonic signal in the time domain using Bitstream Autocorrelation (BACF). It is accurate to a fraction of a cent, runs serially on a per-sample basis with low latency, and is cheap enough (around 50 nanoseconds per sample on a 2016-era laptop) to track many channels at once on a microcontroller.

This page is the high-level tour. The class references that follow document the API: Zero Crossing Collector, Bitstream Autocorrelation, BACF Period Detector, and BACF Pitch Detector.

Zero crossings

The period of a pure tone is easy to read off its zero crossings: a sine crosses zero exactly twice per cycle, so the spacing between crossings is half the period. Real instrument tones are not pure. With strong harmonics the waveform crosses zero many times per cycle, and the raw spacing no longer tells you the period.

Figure 1. A pure tone crosses zero twice per cycle, so the spacing is the period. The same period with strong harmonics crosses zero many times per cycle, and the spacing alone is ambiguous

Heavy low-pass filtering could tame the harmonics, but the phase lag it adds is unacceptable for a low-latency real-time detector. The way out is to stop reading crossing spacing directly and instead look for the repeating pattern in the crossings: autocorrelation.

Bitstream autocorrelation

Autocorrelation measures how much a signal resembles a delayed copy of itself. Standard ACF multiplies the signal by a shifted version of itself and accumulates the products at each lag; it is the most accurate family of time-domain detectors, but the multiply-accumulate over every lag is roughly an O(N²) cost, too much for real-time multi-channel work on small hardware.

BACF keeps the accuracy and discards almost all of the cost by correlating the bitstream rather than the samples. The signal is reduced to one bit per sample, set inside each zero-crossing pulse. With only ones and zeros, the per-lag multiply-accumulate collapses to a bitwise XOR followed by a bit count:

XOR yields a 1 only where the two bitstreams disagree, so the count of set bits after the XOR is a mismatch score: the lower the count, the better the correlation, and a count of zero is a perfect match. One XOR operates on a whole machine word, so 64 samples are compared in a single instruction, and the bit count is a hardware popcount. The result is a mismatch curve whose deepest notch marks the period.

Figure 2. A harmonically rich signal (top), its one-bit-per-sample bitstream (middle), and the XOR mismatch count vs lag (bottom): the count falls to its deepest notch of 0 at the true period

This is the Bitstream Autocorrelation engine. Despite working on a one-bit signal, accuracy is excellent, comparable to floating-point autocorrelation, because the period is ultimately read from the precise zero-crossing timings, not from the coarse bitstream (see The data flow).

Robust where it counts

Because it is autocorrelation at heart, BACF inherits the robustness:

  • Noise. The hysteretic zero-crossing front-end ignores low-level noise, and chaotic crossings from loud noise do not produce a false notch.

  • Missing fundamental. A tone built only from upper harmonics still repeats at the fundamental period, so the notch lands on the true fundamental even when there is no energy at that frequency.

  • Confidence. A normalized periodicity value in [0, 1] falls out of the same computation, so weak or aperiodic input is easy to reject.

Figure 3. Additive noise lifts the mismatch floor, but the deepest notch still lands on the true period
Figure 4. A signal made only of the 2nd, 3rd and 4th harmonics still repeats at the fundamental period; the deepest notch lands there, not on a harmonic

The data flow

The detector is a pipeline of four stages. The first two reusable pieces are public classes; the last two live inside BACF Period Detector.

Figure 5. Zero-crossing edges feed a bitstream correlator, whose result selects the edges that fix the period, which is then turned into a stable frequency

Zero crossing. The Zero Crossing Collector records each crossing with hysteresis (so noise does not trip spurious edges), keeping the pulse height, width, and the samples straddling the crossing. The exact crossing is located to sub-sample precision by interpolating between the two samples, P1 and P2, on either side of zero.

Figure 6. The leading and trailing crossings bound a pulse; each crossing is interpolated between the two samples P1 and P2 that straddle it

Bitset. Once about twice the correlation window of crossings has been collected, the significant pulses (those above 80% of the largest pulse height, which rejects spurious low-level pulses) are packed into a bitset of 64-bit words.

BACF. Bitstream Autocorrelation correlates the bitstream by XOR and popcount, as above.

Sub-collector. Rather than sweeping every lag, the correlation is evaluated only at the candidate periods proposed by pairs of zero-crossing edges. The BACF score is a hint: it selects which edge pair bounds the true cycle. The period is then computed from those edges, interpolated to sub-sample precision, which is more accurate than reading it off the correlation notch. When several candidates correlate well (common at higher frequencies, where many cycles fit the window), the longer period is preferred: more samples and the sub-sample interpolation make it the more precise estimate.

Bias: holding the octave

A note’s harmonic balance shifts as it rings. On a guitar D string, for instance, the second harmonic can grow stronger than the fundamental, and its correlation eventually scores better. Without any memory, the detector would then report a pitch an octave too high.

The bias stage guards against this. It compares each new estimate against the recent history: if a sudden jump matches a harmonic ratio of the established frequency (within a few cents), it keeps the stable frequency rather than chasing the harmonic. A 3-point median then removes isolated spikes while still allowing genuine note changes through.

Figure 7. As a note rings, the 2nd harmonic’s periodicity overtakes the fundamental’s. Without history the reported frequency jumps up an octave at the crossover; the bias holds it on the true fundamental

Period, then pitch

The bias, the median, and the note-shift logic are pitch concerns, not period concerns, so the stack keeps them separate:

Class Role

Zero Crossing Collector

Hysteretic zero-crossing front-end: records each pulse, proposes candidate periods.

Bitstream Autocorrelation

The engine: XOR + popcount autocorrelation of the bitstream.

BACF Period Detector

Builds the bitstream, scores candidates, reconciles harmonics, reports the fundamental period and periodicity.

BACF Pitch Detector

Period to stable frequency: bias against octave jumps, median smoothing, note-shift detection, low-latency prediction.

Performance

On a 2016 MacBook Pro the detector averages about 50 nanoseconds per sample, fast enough for many simultaneous channels in real time, with sub-cent accuracy on sustained notes. The speed comes from sidestepping work: sparse zero-crossing edges instead of every sample, N-bit-parallel XOR instead of scalar multiplies, and a sub-sample period read from the edges instead of an exhaustive lag sweep.

After this method was first published, the author learned of David Warrender’s 1980s hardware patent (since expired) covering a related zero-crossing correlation technique, credited here as independent prior art.

Prediction and zero-latency attacks

Autocorrelation needs at least two cycles to confirm a period, so detection is inherently delayed, and more so for low notes (two cycles of the low E string is about 24 ms). That delay is audible as a latent onset.

The attack itself offers a way out. The leading edge of a plucked or bowed note is a brief, non-periodic transient (pick noise, bow scrape), and the ear cannot resolve pitch from a non-periodic fragment shorter than roughly 20 ms. So during the attack the detector can predict a pitch and start synthesizing immediately, with no audible penalty, achieving effectively zero latency. pitch_detector exposes this through predict_frequency() (and bacf_period_detector::predict_period()).

Low E, tracked with a zero-latency attack

One asymmetry is worth knowing: a predicted pitch that later corrects downward is barely perceptible, while an upward correction is pronounced. Biasing the uncertain initial prediction slightly high keeps the rare error on the inaudible side.

An audible glitch when a low onset is predicted too low and corrects upward
